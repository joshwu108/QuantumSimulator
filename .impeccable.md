## Design Context

### Users
This frontend is for real people using the simulator as a working tool, not
just a one-off classroom screenshot. The core audience is students,
instructors, and technical users who want to build small circuits, compare
noise settings, and understand outcomes quickly without digging through code.

### Brand Personality
Clear, dependable, technical.

The interface should feel like a trustworthy lab instrument: focused,
high-contrast, and calm under experimentation. It should be visually distinct
enough to feel polished, but never so decorative that it slows down reading or
hides the physics.

### Aesthetic Direction
Dark mode by default. The visual language should feel like a modern quantum
workbench rather than a marketing dashboard: deep graphite surfaces, crisp
labels, restrained accent colors, and strong information hierarchy. Emphasis
comes from layout, contrast, and purposeful highlights instead of novelty
effects.

### Design Principles
1. Prioritize legibility over spectacle. Every control and every chart should
   be readable at a glance.
2. Keep the primary workflow obvious: build a circuit, run it, inspect counts,
   probabilities, and Bloch views.
3. Use progressive disclosure. Basic actions stay visible; advanced options
   such as noise, QASM import, and state details live in clear secondary areas.
4. Make feedback immediate. Circuit edits should update the live preview and
   simulation results without extra ceremony.
5. Treat empty, error, and first-run states as teaching moments so the tool
   remains approachable for new users.
